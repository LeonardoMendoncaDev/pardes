*PARDES is a classical acronym from the Jewish hermeneutic tradition. I adopt it here as an operational metaphor, with respect for the original tradition.*

The word *pardes* (פַּרְדֵּס) means, in Hebrew, *orchard* or *garden*. It is the same etymological root that gave rise to the word *paradise*. As an acronym, *PaRDeS* condenses four classical levels of reading the scriptures in Jewish tradition:

- **Peshat** — the literal sense, the surface of the text
- **Remez** — the allusive sense, what the text suggests without explicitly saying
- **Drash** — the interpretive sense, what is discovered through the work of reading
- **Sod** — the mystical or secret sense, unveiled to those who live the text

The application of this acronym to scripture interpretation has been documented for centuries in rabbinical tradition, with particular presence in medieval hermeneutic currents and in Jewish mystical literature. I do not invent it here — I borrow it.

## Why PARDES makes sense for software

The work of building a software system from a human intent has, for those who practice it, four very similar layers. There is what the system is said to be — its *peshat*. There is what it is understood to imply, even if the original purpose did not articulate it — its *remez*. There is what must be decided, written, tested and maintained so that it exists as material reality — its *drash*. And there is what only emerges when the system enters real operation, lives among real users and starts to teach about itself — its *sod*.

The PARDES method explicitly organises these four layers into a chain of fifteen concrete steps, alternating spec and execution. Each layer corresponds to a set of specs and actions. None of the layers disappears — all are always implicit in the reading of a living system. But the explicit detail with which each is written varies with maturity, risk and reversibility in play. The order is taxonomic, not sequential: you advance to a layer when the previous one is stable enough not to generate wrong decisions, and you step back whenever new information shows that a layer behind needs reopening.

## The respect owed to the origin

The Jewish hermeneutic tradition is not mine nor does it belong to me. I adopt its vocabulary because it matches the professional experience I want to describe, not to claim spiritual affiliation or interpretive authority over sacred texts. If you are a reader or practitioner of that tradition and think this appropriation is unfaithful, the channel is open — I accept correction.

What I gain from this metaphor, beyond beauty, is the permanent reminder that the human intent giving rise to a system is, for those who build it, a founding text. And that faithful interpretation of a founding text does not exhaust itself at the surface: it demands patience, method and humility to unveil layers.
